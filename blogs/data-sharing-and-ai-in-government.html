Data Sharing and AI in Government — The Same Problem, Twice

I've now worked on data sharing projects across multiple government organisations — DfE, NHS England, GDS, and others — across the better part of a decade. The outcomes have been mixed. Some of those projects delivered something real and lasting. Others stalled, got deprioritised, or quietly died. I've been reflecting on why, and why it matters more now than it ever has — because everything government wants to do with AI has the same problem sitting underneath it.

Why data sharing keeps coming up

Data sharing appears in nearly every digital transformation agenda in government, and the reason is simple: most government services are only as good as the data that underpins them. If a citizen has to provide the same information multiple times to different departments, that's a failure. If a frontline worker can't see the data they need to make a good decision, that's a failure. The potential upside is enormous — better services, reduced burden, faster processing, more accurate decisions. It also has explicit political backing — data sharing featured in Labour's manifesto and has been part of their reform agenda in government. The question now isn't whether there's appetite at the top, it's whether that intent is translating into the sustained, cross-departmental commitment that actually gets things built.

Why it's so hard

The technical challenges are real but they're rarely the biggest problem. The harder challenges are human ones.

Trust is the first. Organisations are often reluctant to share data with other organisations because they fear losing control, getting blamed for misuse, or creating dependencies they can't manage. This is sometimes presented as a legal concern, but in my experience the legal frameworks are more permissive than people assume — it's the cultural willingness to share that's the bottleneck.

Ownership and accountability is the second. Data sharing across organisational boundaries immediately raises the question of who owns the problem. When something goes wrong, who is responsible? When priorities conflict, whose takes precedence? Without clear answers, projects drift or stall.

Varying levels of readiness is the third. A data sharing project is only as strong as the least mature participant. If one organisation has well-structured, well-governed data and another is working from spreadsheets and legacy systems, the integration is incredibly difficult. The gap in technical maturity between different parts of government is still significant.

AI makes this more urgent, not less

Here's the thing that's been sitting with me recently. Every meaningful AI use case in government depends on data — and often on data that sits across organisational boundaries. If you want AI to help a frontline worker make a better decision, it needs to see relevant data from multiple systems. If you want AI to identify someone who needs support before they reach crisis point, it needs joined-up data. AI doesn't create a data problem in government — it reveals one that was already there.

What I've noticed, and what I think Hippo Digital articulates well in their thinking on this, is that organisations with technically strong foundations are still stalling on AI — not because of the data, but because of decision paralysis. Nobody can agree on who owns the deployment decision, what risks are acceptable, or who funds the work once it moves beyond the transformation team. These are exactly the same conversations I've been having in data sharing projects for years. The problem has a different label on it, but it's the same problem.

If we don't get data sharing right, government's AI ambitions are going to hit exactly the same wall that data sharing projects have hit. Lots of pilots. Lots of enthusiasm. Stalling when it comes to production.

What failure looks like

The projects I've seen fail — on data sharing and increasingly on AI — tend to share common features. They were initiated from the top with insufficient understanding of the operational complexity. They underestimated the work needed to align incentives across organisations. They tried to solve everything at once rather than finding a meaningful first use case that could demonstrate value quickly. Or they got stuck in governance loops, with committees producing documents rather than teams producing working software.

With AI there's an added wrinkle: a successful pilot is particularly dangerous. It creates the impression of progress without creating the conditions for scale. I've seen organisations complete genuinely impressive AI pilots that then stalled for twelve months because nobody had thought about procurement, information governance, legal sign-off, or operational ownership. Those things can't be an afterthought — by the time the pilot succeeds, it's already too late to bring them in cleanly.

What success looks like

The projects that have worked — data sharing and AI alike — started small and proved value quickly against a clearly defined, shared problem. Not a vague ambition to "share data better" or "use AI," but a specific workflow that was genuinely broken and that all parties wanted to fix.

They also treated governance, IG, legal, security, and procurement as delivery partners from the start — not gatekeepers to negotiate with at the end. The difference between those two postures is enormous. When the people who need to sign things off are involved in designing the process, you end up with clear controls and predictable timelines rather than a long queue of surprises at the end. Sign-off theatre — where you go through the motions of governance without genuinely engaging it — is one of the most reliable ways to kill a project.

And critically, they were ruthless about what counted as progress. A small deployment that changes a real workflow — that a real person uses every day — is worth more than ten polished demos. It builds the trust, the institutional knowledge, and the shared understanding that the next phase needs. In data sharing terms: one working data exchange that genuinely improves a service outcome is more valuable than a comprehensive data strategy document that nobody acts on.

What I hope happens next

We're far enough into this government's cycle now to start asking harder questions. The commitment to data sharing and digital reform was clear coming in. Some of that has translated into real action — there are programmes running, investment flowing, and genuine momentum in parts of the system. But there are also familiar patterns emerging: good intent meeting the friction of organisational complexity, governance processes that haven't kept up with ambition, and the perennial challenge of sustaining cross-departmental work when everyone has their own priorities and pressures.

The same is true of AI. The interest is real, the investment is real, and some of the pilots are genuinely impressive. But we're at the point in the cycle where pilots need to become programmes, and that's where things have historically broken down — in government on data sharing, and increasingly on AI everywhere. The conditions for success are known. The question is whether we have the patience and the structural commitment to create them.

My hope is that the lessons learned from a decade of data sharing projects — start small, get governance in early, prove value in real workflows, build trust before you build scale — get applied to the AI agenda before we go through the same cycle of excitement and stall again. We know what good looks like. The challenge, as always, is doing it.
